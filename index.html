Research Interests

I have been exploring the domain of wearable robotics, using both traditional rigid exoskeletons and new soft exosuits. My interest is in developing cutting edge technology for functional movement assistance and remote monitoring. Wearable robotics is a very challenging field and the current technology is still far from being ready to everyday use. However, it is reasonable to think that these robots are going to play an important role in our future. Robots that can help people, reduce injuries, improve recovery during rehabilitation, monitor body parameters and early diagnose potential diseases, compensate physical disability in an efficient way, or even "just" assist during fatiguing everyday tasks at home: make life easier!

There are several aspects of the physical human-robot interaction that still need futher investigation (e.g. complex mechanical coupling with the human body) but I like to focus my efforts on:

  • how to design novel solutions to support human movements,
  • how to smartly share the control of the body between the human user and the robot,
  • how to detect and anticipate the user intentions of movement,
  • how to adapt the robot initiative to the different applications and contexts,
  • how to collect meaningful data from the human-robot interaction.

Being an engineer, I'm very interested in solutions that can have a real impact on people, translating out of the academic environment. Thus I'm always considering themes like cost-efficiency (e.g. underactuation and minimal amount of sensors), robustness (test unsupervised users's behaviors in unconstrained environments), and proven usefulness (e.g. investigating the effects of a control strategy but also how to properly assess these effects) in my daily activity.

Video presentation of our RA-L paper [J5] at ICRA 2021. In this work, we presented a new sensing and control strategy for our soft wearable robot, tested on 8 healthy individuals. Main results: no restriction in ROM, accurate tracking of free motions, reduction of almost 60% of muscle activity when assisted, and ability to reproduce a recorded trajectory.

Harvard soft wearable robot to assist shoulder and elbow movements. The robot uses pneumatic textile-based actuators to assist both joints. In this picture, validation of the design of the robot with healthy lab members.
