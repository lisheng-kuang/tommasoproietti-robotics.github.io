Research Interest

I have been exploring the domain of wearable robotics, using both traditional rigid exoskeletons and new soft exosuits. The lab I'm setting up wants to develop cutting-edge technology for functional movement assistance and remote monitoring. Wearable robotics is a very challenging field and the current technology is still far from being ready to everyday use. However, it is reasonable to think that these robots are going to play an important role in our future. Robots that can help people, reduce injuries, improve recovery during rehabilitation, monitor body parameters and early diagnose potential diseases, efficiently compensate for physical disability, or even "just" assist during fatiguing everyday tasks at home: make life easier!

Several aspects of the physical human-robot interaction still need further investigation (e.g. complex mechanical coupling with the human body) but we will focus our efforts on:

  • how to design novel solutions to support human movements,
  • how to smartly share the control of the body between the human user and the robot,
  • how to detect and anticipate the user's intentions of movement,
  • how to adapt the robot initiative to the different applications and contexts,
  • how to collect meaningful data from the human-robot interaction.

Being an engineer, I'm very interested in solutions that can have a real impact on people, translating out of the academic environment. Thus I'm always considering themes like cost-efficiency (e.g. underactuation and minimal amount of sensors), robustness (testing unsupervised users' behaviors in unconstrained environments), and proven usefulness (e.g. investigating the effects of a control strategy but also how to properly assess these effects) in my daily activity.

Gallery

Video presentation for our RA-L paper [J6]. We showed the good performance achieved by our kinematics-based controller for a soft wearable shoulder robot for assisting industrial tasks.

Supplementary material for our Science Trans Med paper [J9]. By using a soft wearable robot, we demonstrated improved motor and functional ability in 10 individuals living with ALS. With only 4kg of weight on the waist, an intuitive control strategy and a battery-powered, fully untethered architecture, this work opens up to future use out of lab environment.

Receiving the Cullen Eduction and Research Fund (CERF) Medical Engineering Prize 2022 with Prof. Conor Walsh, and our collabolators from Brown University/Braingate Prof. Leigh Hochberg and Dr. Jacob Gusman.

Video presentation of our RA-L paper [J5] at ICRA 2021. In this work, we presented a new sensing and control strategy for our soft wearable robot, tested on 8 healthy individuals. Main results: no restriction in ROM, accurate tracking of free motions, reduction of almost 60% of muscle activity when assisted, and ability to reproduce a recorded trajectory.

Harvard soft wearable robot to assist shoulder and elbow movements. The robot uses pneumatic textile-based actuators to assist both joints. In this picture, validation of the design of the robot with healthy lab members.

Video submitted with RA-L paper [J4] showing the usage of our soft wearable robot to assist an occupational therapist performing rehabilitation exercises with 5 stroke individuals.

ABLE, UPMC 4-DOF exoskeleton for upper-limb assistance. ABLE actively controls 3DOF of the shoulder and the elbow flexion/extension. In the picture, the user is performing a reaching task towards a 7-DOF WAM manipulator as target (part of my [J3] work).

Simulation of a dynamic nonprehensile manipulation task (dynamic grasp to roll), part of my MS thesis work at Northwestern University. In red the manipulator, in blue the object, in green the target pose, in dashed green the transition pose between dynamic grasp and roll, in black the friction cone.

Video submitted with JNER paper [J3] showing motor adaptation and subsequent after-effects occurring in a participant as consequence of the prolonged exposition to the robotic force field.
